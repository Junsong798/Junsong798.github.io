Research Projects

Manuscript Under Review

Wang, X.T., Wang, P., Lu, J., Zhou, J., Li, G., & Garelik, S. Anticipatory Emotions Guide Future-Oriented Choices and Precautionary Behaviour during COVID-19

  • We synthesized several influential theories concerning the roles of emotion in decision-making and developed a hypothesis of anticipatory emotions as information for social risks. We define anticipatory emotions as any discrete emotions induced from the expectation of possible future outcomes and felt at the time of decision-making. We predict that as an essential part of emotional intelligence, anticipatory emotions help decision-makers assess risks under uncertainty. In an online study, anticipatory emotions were induced and appraised by asking participants to rate various emotions they would feel if they were infected by COVID-19. We examined how anticipatory emotions, in comparison to present emotion appraisal, regulate intertemporal choices and precautious behaviors of healthy volunteers (N = 491) during the most stringent period of the COVID-19 “lockdown” in China. Compared to those who were asked to evaluate and report their present feelings, participants in the anticipatory emotion group revealed a higher future-oriented preference with a significantly lower delay discounting rate. Anticipatory emotions also enhanced the likelihood of the participants engaging in proactive, precautionary behaviors against COVID-19. This effect was mediated by risk perception as measured by the subjective likelihood of being infected. Together, the results suggest that anticipatory emotions promote future-oriented, rational decision-making.

Manuscript Under Preparation

Wang, X.T., Lu, J., & Dominguez , J. The Interplay between Explicit and Implicit Face-Based Attributions on Leader Preference: Take the 2016 U.S. Presidential Election as a Lens

  • Previous studies suggest that personal perception based on a glance of faces can accurately predict electoral outcomes. However, it is unclear whether mere face-based attributions remain a valid predictor when factors beyond facial cues are involved and how they are intertwined. Using the 2016 U.S. election as a lens, we examined the difference between explicit face (when a candidate was identified in a morphed face of the candidate and a control figure) and implicit face (when the candidate was not identified) attributions and how they interplay in forming leadership preferences in a pre- (Study 1) and a post-election sample (Study 2). Our results show that once the candidate was identified, the participants diverged in explicit but not implicit attributions on both leadership and likability (Study 1). Furthermore, explicit but not implicit leadership ratings accurately predict electoral choice of the participants, indicating that factors beyond mere face-based preferences played a significant role in determining explicit ratings as well as political preferences. Aside from mere face-based appearance, we found that gender preference and personal characteristics significantly predicted explicit ratings. In the post-election sample (Study 2), we showed that explicit ratings could be further influenced by the negativity bias, since the implicit-to-explicit reduction was more evident for the Clinton composites than the Trump composites among people holding a neutral political stance. Interestingly, in both samples, it is the loser (Clinton) that has more positive face-based attributions. Combining the results, the interplay between explicit and implicit attributions is discussed.

Lu, J., & Wang, X.T. Separating Loss Aversion from Ownership in the Endowment Effect

  • The endowment effect has been commonly attributed to loss aversion. However, an alternative ownership account maintains that people are reluctant to give up a good they own because of the value-added to the good due to the possessing experience. We evaluated these two accounts using the parameterization of loss aversion (Tversky & Kahneman, 1992) and willingness to accept (WTA) to measure the extent of loss aversion and the effects of ownership experience with a good through a series of choices between a sure thing and a risky option. In Study 1, participants experienced a change in endowment status of a pen (from the original pen to a more expensive or cheaper pen). This ownership experience influenced the liking of the good but not the loss aversion parameter derived from their choices. In Study 2, without ownership changes, the loss aversion parameter varied as a function of initial price of the endowment. Loss aversion was higher when the proportion rather than the absolute amount of gains and losses increased, Study 3 demonstrated that WTA was higher after an ownership change, either winning or losing, while the loss aversion parameter remained unchanged. Therefore, we dissociated the effects of loss aversion and ownership experience, which are subjected to different decision factors. Ownership experiences enhanced valuation of a good while not affecting the degree of loss aversion. These results suggest that while loss aversion is a ubiquitous phenomenon, the ownership account was more applicable for explaining the endowment effect.

Ongoing and Participated Projects

Team leader of the ongoing project “Making Intertemporal Choices as if There is No Tomorrow: a Meta-Analytic Review of the Age Effect on Delay Discounting”.

  • The empirical findings on the age differences in delay discounting are decidedly mixed over the past three decades, and the theories of various fields make differential predictions. In this review, we aimed to examine the overall effect of age on temporal discount rates and to detect potential moderators. Within the framework of meta-analysis, I assume that the discount rate is a differentiable function with respect to age and associated effect sizes with the function’s first derivative using Lagrange’s mean value theorem. The recovered first derivative function suggested a quadratic relationship between age and delay discounting rate, lending credence to the evolutionary model of Sozou and Seymour (2003). Subsequent analyses suggested that socioeconomic status, delay discounting models, publication year, and incentive scheme significantly moderated the age effect. Moreover, publication bias, time-lag bias, and incompatible methods were found to blur the true relationship between age and temporal discount rates. Theoretical, methodological, and practical implications were discussed.

Investigation on the Crime Prediction based on Life History Theory

  • As a middle-level evolutionary theory, Life History Theory suggests that individual differences are partially rooted in childhood environments. Individuals brought up in a harsh and uncertain environment are more likely to be impulsive, risk-taking, as well as being willing to engage in criminal behavior. I analyzed life history data, risk preferences, and intertemporal choice data collected from sentenced inmates from a security prison in China. Using exploratory data analysis, I predicted sentence and crime types using XGBoost (an ensemble method in machine learning). To achieve higher predictive accuracy in the 10-fold cross-validation, a grid search was used to identify the best parameters like shrinkage and stopping rules. Result indicated a large test mean squared error in the prediction of the sentence, which may be due to the influence of the law and the presumption of crime. Life history indicators and risk preferences classified types of crime obtained an average accuracy of 84%. A more explicable method, the C5.0 decision tree, indicated that impulsive criminals have a sizable delayed discounting rate and better family backgrounds, while conspiracy criminals are more inclined to take moral risks than other criminals.

My Story

Like most of my peers in high school, I studied a similar set of curricula and was not aware of my own passion for psychology, which I felt was a from of therapy. However, life is a journey with many unexpected twists and turns. Things changed when I accidentally watched The Brain (最强大脑), which is a scientific reality that allows people to display their exceptional brainpower. As a scientific judge and a professor in the department of psychology at PKU, Dr. Wei Kunlin has introduced concepts that underlie these prodigious feats, such as working memory, mental representation, stereopsis etc., which made a lasting impression on me about what psychology is and how interesting it can be.

I was crazy about watching The Brain through the last two years of my undergraduate, and I seized every opportunity to learn about basic concepts in cognitive psychology through online forums about this reality and the relevant books of K. Stanovich, D. Kahneman, H. Simon, and A. Ericsson, for the simple reason to be like the people in the reality and be more efficient in learning. It wasn’t long before I found that, however, the brain does not seem not to be a general purpose computer, because it is insensitive to mathematics rather than language, it memorized people other than our country harder, it has not developed enough capacity to deal with overwhelming information in this current, ever-complex world.

In my senior year, a book by D. Maestripieri on evolutionary psychology offered me a direct glance at my quest. It said that human behavior is hardwired in our brains and evolved during the Pleistocene, when our ancestors lived as hunter-gatherers. I have since followed this perspective, becoming familiar with the work of many researchers, and have realized my deep passion: I desired to understand how human decisions and behaviors were influenced by ancestral histories and ecological cues, even when environments are mismatched today.

Having found my engineering internships mind-numbingly dull, I turned down a graduate recommendation and applied for a psychology programme. At that moment, I seemed to pluck up my courage, along with the thunder, knocked a hole in Shawshank’s underground pipe. I was luck enough to be accepted into applied psychology at CUHKsz and began my research with a professor of decision-making and evolutionary psychology.

Starting with my graduate thesis project, I tried to separate loss aversion and psychological attachment in the endowment effect. I participated in this behavioral economics program because I realized that the money and resources we have today are modern derivatives of food, and thus our economic decisions can be influenced by our foraging strategies and risk perception. I first linked the paradigm of cumulative prospect theory to endowment processes. However, I quickly became overwhelmed by the axiomatic model and had no idea how to program it in R. Treading in unfamiliar territory, it was impossible to seek help. I had no choice but to help myself by reading statistics and neuroeconomics books one after another and searching information on Stack Overflow. Finally, after trudging forward day after day for about half a year, I modified the parameterization of CPT and finished writing my program in R after discussing my scripts with statistics professors and PKU postdocs. What I learned from this experience is that, from the perspective of social constructivism, doing research is a process of creating knowledge in a social community that requires communication and practice with different people rather than the romanticized notion of a lone scholar doodling on blank sheets of notebook paper.

To enrich my research experience, I became a visiting graduate of Shenzhen Research Institute of Big Data (SRIBD) through interviews and conducting the Smart Campus project with students from different disciplines. I spent a lot of time reading papers and books on behavioral addiction given the specific task of identifying factors that can cause Internet Gaming Disorder (IGD) and low self-management. Unlike others, I argued that IGD is a moral panic caused by excessive worry, and I supported my argument using several thought experiments based on the perspective of ego depletion that posits self-control as a finite resource: suppose there is no distraction in school, and thus will be no reduction in ego resources, and then one could argue that every student will not become addicted to games. In fact, many students in tightly controlled high schools still cross school walls and go to Internet cafes. The result of our experiments supported the claim that self-control is not a immune system as suggested by ego depletion. Using the PC algorithm to analyze the IGD scale and related psychological traits, it turned out that self-control was not a direct cause of IGD. Moreover, study/game efficacy blocked the effect of self-control in a hierarchical regression. This argument is in line with life history theory. That is, low self-control is a slow strategy to maximize fitness in a particular environment, rather than an immune system preventing us from playing game indiscriminately. Based on self-determination theory, I explained that whether students play games or learn depends on whether they are granted with basic need of autonomous and competence, which means the human brain is not entirely plastic, but has deep-rooted specific psychological needs. In my year in SRIBD, in addition to machine learning and causal analysis I learned from both SRIBD and CMU professors, I learned new insights from the perspective of evolutionary psychology, that we should have more tolerance and empathy for impulsive people, because their choice is partially determined by the environment.

At the end of my second year, I still benefit from my interdisciplinary research. At the time, I tried to explore ramifications of different life history strategies using psychometric techniques and machine learning. In my course project, I investigated the factor structure and nomological network of the Chinese version of Mini-K scale, which is one of the most prevalent tools for assessing life history strategies. That was the first research that I realized that there could be stark inconsistencies between theory and data that could further be used to refine the theory. The most significant finding is that the slow strategy does not always result in less mating effort, but may have higher scores in some indirect mating efforts, suggesting that a tradeoff between general mating effort versus parental effort it is oversimplified. I also successfully fixed the Haywood case in the CFA model by combining two items on 7-point into 5-point Likert scale, which made me understand the limitations of Likert scale: nonlinear metrics and easily skewed. On the other hand, I used ensemble learning in collaboration with SRIBD to predict the criminal behavior with different life history indicators. During that time, I began to study scripts on GitHub and Kaggle, which enriched my immature programming skills.
